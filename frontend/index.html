Bone

14 years and 9 years since Bitcoin and Ethereum are conceived respectively, money safeguarded by trusted institution is still seen as safer compared to the trustless alternatives.
This can be seen from the 7x outstanding circulation of USDC compared to DAI. [1]

The vision of self-custody peer-to-peer money is still far from fully realized, as even risk-seeking Defi users still prefer custodial money, never mind
the risk-averse users in the real world.

My hypothesis for what one of the root causes may be is non-Custodial money risks outweigh benefits.
Non-custodial money has no recourse against a long list of potential exploits.
When there is no legal recourse, your position can get wiped out if one of the long list of risks materializes from e.g. governance, collateral, smart contract, and
censorship.
By contrast, a US regulated custodial money like USDC won't keep you up at night in comparison - a dollar on-chain, a dollar in a bank/90-day US bond,
and regular 3rd party audits.

So why even bother with non-custodial money?
From history, institutions and their monies eventually fail, history tells us most monies within last 200 years ago failed. People need safety outside of the safety
net provided by institutions, traditionally, gold's played this role.

Which point in the design space for money will a non-custodial money be safer than custodial money, as a store hold of wealth?
I am talking the kind of money that you can buy and forget, and you can feel comfortable it will be there for you in the most stressful moments of social chaos. [4]

A simple non-custodial gold synthetic can serve this role.

Each gold synthetic coin, bXAU (Bone Gold), is over-collateralized by at least 200% of the equivalent in DAI:
you can redeem the equivalent amount of DAI for gold at the oracle price at any time.

Furthermore, I believe the implementation should follow the principles below:

1. Extreme simplicity. [5]

- Simple stability mechanic: there are only 2 functions with write access to the contract storage - mint and redeem - meant to allow
arbitragaers to mint and redeem against the DAI in reserve and the reserve contract will honour the oracle price of gold upon redemption.

- Simple implementation: the synthetic contract has in the order of about 200 lines of code, and it is possible to stay this small.

2. Resilience to tail risks.

- DAI's diversified balance sheet should help hedge against any 1 of its collateral collapsing.

However, it may make sense to further diversify the reserve against:
a. DAI being a single-point-of-failure,
b. USDC's >50% as colateral on Maker's balance sheet thus higher censorship/custodial risk, and
c. emergency shutdown prioritizing Vault holders.

For this reason, I chose 200% collateralization ratio and 1% mint/redeem fee to protect stablecoin holders.
Furthermore, I think it would make sense to iteratively add more stablecoin collaterals like LUSD.
